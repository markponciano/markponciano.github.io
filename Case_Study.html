Case Study

From manual chaos to systems that run themselves

How I rebuilt the back office of a growing UK street-food and events business, starting in my first few weeks.

Mark Lorenze Ponciano · Systems, Automation & Bookkeeping for SMBs

The business

A multi-venue street-food and events caterer, running food concessions at football stadiums alongside a busy corporate and private events side. Growing quickly, and like a lot of businesses that grow quickly, the back office hadn't kept pace with the front.

The problem

On paper the business was doing well. Underneath, the admin was held together by spreadsheets, memory, and people staying late:

  • Payroll was worked out by hand every week, for a rotating crew of contractors and virtual assistants, in more than one currency.
  • The weekly P&L wasn't fully trusted. It ran on a cost setup that had quietly drifted away from what the accounts actually said.
  • Quotes and invoices were re-typed by hand between separate tools, with the usual typos, delays and version confusion.
  • GoHighLevel, the CRM, was sold as "it does everything," but its automation was brittle, hard to see into, and kept breaking at the worst moments.
  • Bookings, trailers and calendars lived in different places that didn't always agree, so things occasionally got double-booked or missed.

How I approached it

I don't walk in and start building. I do the books and the day-to-day first, because that's where the real problems show themselves. Reconciling the numbers and running the weekly work is the fastest way to see exactly where the money leaks, where the time goes, and which "system" is really just someone remembering to do a thing. Then I fix the most painful thing first, prove it works, and move on to the next one. Nothing fancier than it needs to be.

What I did

Week 3

Stopped the matchday bleeding

Within three weeks of joining, I replaced the by-hand matchday process with a single living spreadsheet that took in stadium food-sales and worked out subcontractor pay automatically. It changed how the team ran every match day from then on.

Found money

Surfaced ~£80k hiding in the P&L

I reconciled the weekly P&L's cost setup against the actual accounts and found about £80k a year (annualised) in costs the model had been quietly missing. The business had been reading its profit as higher than it really was. Now the numbers tell the truth.

Automation

Turned quotes into invoices on their own

Before the business even had a CRM, I built its first automated quote-to-invoice pipeline, wiring together the tools it already used so an enquiry could become an invoice without anyone re-typing anything.

The engine

Built the system the business actually runs on

I designed and built the system the business runs on: bookings, quoting, invoicing and payments, end to end. The brittle automation moved onto solid, reliable ground that can be checked and trusted, instead of breaking at the worst moment.

Reliability

Kept the weekly finances running like clockwork

Payroll, P&L and stadium-sales invoicing, every week without a gap, so the owners always knew exactly where they stood. And when a colleague left a half-built tool behind, I picked it up and shipped it rather than let it rot.

The results
